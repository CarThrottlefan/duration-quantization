The Snap Line: why some notes round more than others

Note symbols can only represent certain durations. A played note gets rounded to the nearest one — but those representable durations are not evenly spaced, so the rounding error isn't either. The paper states this in one sentence and shows two brackets in a figure; this makes the rounding itself something you can drag through.

Drag the handle below to "play" a duration. Toggle the modifiers to see how dots, ties, and triplets fill in the gaps — and watch the trade-off the paper names directly: more representable values means less error, but a busier, harder-to-read line.

The shaded bands are catchment zones — the range of durations that round to that tick. Wider band = more leeway. Notice quarter's band is built to be exactly twice the width of eighth's: the paper's claim, made measurable.

The rounding error, as a continuous function

The paper never plots this — it only states that error depends on tempo, target duration, and instrument. Here it is as a shape: a sawtooth, not a flat line. Each tooth's height is exactly half its zone's width above. Add modifiers and watch the teeth shrink — that's the precision you're trading for a busier line.

Practice History & Quantization Drift

Small deviations accumulate over time. This heatmap tracks your recent inputs (X-axis = duration, Y-axis = attempt history). Notice how your raw inputs might scatter, but the system forces them into rigid columns—sometimes amplifying the mistake if they cross a catchment zone boundary.

Why this matters beyond this one figure: Section IV-A's note-duration design ends by asking whether the "too short / good / too long" threshold should depend on tempo, target duration, or the musician's skill. This line is the mechanism behind that question — the zones above are exactly the regions a threshold-based assessor has to draw, and they are provably uneven. Any fixed percentage threshold treats a wide zone (quarter) and a narrow one (eighth) identically, even though one allows far more real timing slack than the other.
